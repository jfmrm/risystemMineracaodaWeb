<h1 class="title mathjax"><span class="descriptor">Title:</span>
Distributed Weight Consolidation: A Brain Segmentation Case Study</h1>
<div class="authors"><span class="descriptor">Authors:</span>
<a href="/search/cs?searchtype=author&query=McClure%2C+P">Patrick McClure</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs?searchtype=author&query=Zheng%2C+C+Y">Charles Y. Zheng</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs?searchtype=author&query=Kaczmarzyk%2C+J+R">Jakub R. Kaczmarzyk</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs?searchtype=author&query=Rogers-Lee%2C+J">John Rogers-Lee</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs?searchtype=author&query=Ghosh%2C+S+S">Satrajit S. Ghosh</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs?searchtype=author&query=Nielson%2C+D">Dylan Nielson</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs?searchtype=author&query=Bandettini%2C+P">Peter Bandettini</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs?searchtype=author&query=Pereira%2C+F">Francisco Pereira</a></div>
<div class="dateline">(Submitted on 28 May 2018 (<a href="/abs/1805.10863v1">v1</a>), last revised 17 Sep 2018 (this version, v2))</div>
<blockquote class="abstract mathjax">
<span class="descriptor">Abstract:</span> Collecting the large datasets needed to train deep neural networks can be
very difficult, particularly for the many applications for which sharing and
pooling data is complicated by practical, ethical, or legal concerns. However,
it may be the case that derivative datasets or predictive models developed
within individual sites can be shared and combined with fewer restrictions.
Training on distributed data and combining the resulting networks is often
viewed as continual learning, but these methods require networks to be trained
sequentially. In this paper, we introduce distributed weight consolidation
(DWC), a continual learning method to consolidate the weights of separate
neural networks, each trained on an independent dataset. We evaluated DWC with
a brain segmentation case study, where we consolidated dilated convolutional
neural networks trained on independent structural magnetic resonance imaging
(sMRI) datasets from different sites. We found that DWC led to increased
performance on held-out test sets from the different sites, as well as on a
very large and completely independent multi-site dataset. Moreover, performance
was close to that obtained by training a single network on data from all sites.
</blockquote>
