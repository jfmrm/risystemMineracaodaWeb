<h1 class="title mathjax"><span class="descriptor">Title:</span>
Online Learning for Position-Aided Millimeter Wave Beam Training</h1>
<div class="authors"><span class="descriptor">Authors:</span>
<a href="/search/cs,math?searchtype=author&query=Va%2C+V">Vutha Va</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs,math?searchtype=author&query=Shimizu%2C+T">Takayuki Shimizu</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs,math?searchtype=author&query=Bansal%2C+G">Gaurav Bansal</a>, 
<a href="/search/cs,math?searchtype=author&query=Heath%2C+R+W">Robert W. Heath Jr</a></div>
<div class="dateline">(Submitted on 9 Sep 2018 (<a href="/abs/1809.03014v1">v1</a>), last revised 12 Sep 2018 (this version, v2))</div>
<blockquote class="abstract mathjax">
<span class="descriptor">Abstract:</span> Accurate beam alignment is essential for beam-based millimeter wave
communications. Conventional beam sweeping solutions often have large overhead,
which is unacceptable for mobile applications like vehicle-to-everything.
Learning-based solutions that leverage sensor data like position to identify
good beam directions are one approach to reduce the overhead. Most existing
solutions, though, are supervised-learning where the training data is collected
beforehand. In this paper, we use a multi-armed bandit framework to develop
online learning algorithms for beam pair selection and refinement. The beam
pair selection algorithm learns coarse beam directions in some predefined beam
codebook, e.g., in discrete angles separated by the 3 dB beamwidths. The beam
refinement fine-tunes the identified directions to match the peak of the power
angular spectrum at that position. The beam pair selection uses the upper
confidence bound (UCB) with a newly proposed risk-aware feature, while the beam
refinement uses a modified optimistic optimization algorithm. The proposed
algorithms show a fast learning behavior. When using 16x16 arrays at both the
transmitter and receiver, it can achieve on average 1 dB gain over the
exhaustive search (over 271x271 beam pairs) on the unrefined codebook within
100 time-steps with a training budget of only 30 beam pairs.
</blockquote>
